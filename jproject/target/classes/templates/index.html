1. What is this prediction for?
This prediction helps determine if a C-section might be necessary based on input data.
2. What factors are considered in the prediction?
The prediction considers fetal distress, maternal age, gestational age, previous C-section history, blood pressure, and BMI.
3. What is Fetal Distress?
Fetal distress refers to signs that a baby is not getting enough oxygen during pregnancy or labor. It is often detected through abnormal heart rate patterns.
4. What is Maternal Age?
Maternal age is the age of the mother during pregnancy. Advanced maternal age (above 45) may increase pregnancy-related risks, including the need for a C-section.
5. What is Gestational Age?
Gestational age is the number of weeks a baby has been developing in the womb, starting from the mother’s last menstrual period. A normal pregnancy lasts around 40 weeks.
6. What does Previous C-section mean?
This refers to whether the mother has had a C-section in a previous pregnancy. A history of C-section increases the likelihood of requiring another one.
7. What is Blood Pressure?
Blood pressure is the force of blood against the walls of arteries. High blood pressure during pregnancy (above 140/90 mmHg) can lead to complications requiring a C-section.
8. What is BMI (Body Mass Index)?
BMI is a measure of body fat based on weight and height. A very high or low BMI can affect pregnancy health and delivery method.
9. How does Fetal Distress impact C-section decisions?
Fetal distress can lead to an emergency C-section if the baby is not receiving enough oxygen or is in danger.
10. How does Maternal Age influence the need for a C-section?
Older mothers (above 45) have a higher risk of complications like high blood pressure, diabetes, and difficulty in natural labor, increasing the chances of a C-section.
11. Why is Gestational Age important in predicting a C-section?
Babies born too early (before 37 weeks) or too late (after 42 weeks) may face complications that require a C-section for a safe delivery.
12. How does a Previous C-section affect future deliveries?
Having a prior C-section increases the chances of another one due to risks like uterine rupture if a vaginal birth is attempted.
13. Why is Blood Pressure monitored during pregnancy?
High blood pressure can cause complications like preeclampsia, which may require an early or emergency C-section to protect both mother and baby.
14. How does BMI affect pregnancy and C-section chances?
A high BMI (obesity) can make labor difficult, increasing the need for a C-section, while a very low BMI may indicate undernutrition risks.
